How It Works

The Inherited Property
Coordination Process

A simplified overview of the key stages involved in settling an estate and transferring title to heirs in the Philippines. Every family's situation is different — this is the typical path for an uncomplicated estate.

1

Gather Personal Documents

Death certificate, marriage certificate, birth certificates of all heirs, and valid IDs. These establish the legal foundation of the estate and identify all rightful heirs.

2

Gather Property Documents

Transfer Certificate of Title (TCT) or Condominium Certificate of Title (CCT), Tax Declaration, and Real Property Tax receipts. Missing documents can often be reconstituted.

3

Determine the Settlement Path

If there is no will and all heirs are of legal age and in agreement, an Extra-Judicial Settlement (EJS) is typically the fastest and most cost-effective route. If a will exists, court probate is required.

4

Property Valuation

A licensed appraiser assesses the Fair Market Value of the property. This is used to compute the estate tax liability.

5

Prepare and Execute the EJS Document

The Extra-Judicial Settlement is drafted by a lawyer, signed by all heirs (with an apostilled SPA for those abroad), notarised, and published in a newspaper of general circulation for three consecutive weeks.

6

File and Pay Estate Tax at BIR

The estate tax return is filed and estate tax is paid at the Bureau of Internal Revenue. The BIR then issues a Certificate Authorizing Registration (CAR) — required for title transfer.

7

Pay Transfer Tax at LGU

Transfer tax is paid at the Local Government Unit (City or Municipality) where the property is located. This triggers issuance of the Tax Clearance.

8

Transfer Title at Registry of Deeds

The new Transfer Certificate of Title is issued in the names of the heirs. The estate is now legally settled and the property is ready to be retained, rented, or sold.

Private Client Insight

The timeline above assumes a straightforward estate with cooperative heirs and complete documents. The most common causes of delay are: missing or unlocatable documents, overseas heirs without an apostilled SPA, unpaid real property taxes, and estate tax not previously filed. Our assessment identifies these issues before they become expensive surprises.

  • Death Certificate of the deceased (PSA-authenticated)
  • Marriage Certificate — if married (PSA)
  • Birth Certificates of all heirs (PSA)
  • Valid IDs of all heirs
  • Apostilled SPA — for heirs based abroad
  • Last Will and Testament — if one exists
Property Documents
  • Transfer Certificate of Title (TCT) or CCT
  • Tax Declaration (updated)
  • Real Property Tax receipts (current)
  • Tax Clearance from LGU
  • Survey Plan / Lot Plan
  • Deed of Sale (if previously purchased)
Legal & Settlement Documents
  • Extra-Judicial Settlement of Estate (if no will)
  • Publication affidavit — EJS published for 3 weeks
  • Court Order — if probate is required
  • Deed of Partition — if estate is to be divided
  • Deed of Absolute Sale — if selling after settlement
BIR & LGU Requirements
  • Estate Tax Return (BIR Form 1801)
  • Certificate Authorizing Registration (CAR) from BIR
  • Transfer Tax receipt from LGU
  • Tax Clearance Certificate from LGU
  • New Tax Declaration — in heir's name
  • New Transfer Certificate of Title — Registry of Deeds

Understanding Estate Tax
in the Philippines

"Estate tax must be settled before any property can be legally transferred — and the clock starts the moment a property owner passes away."

Under the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN) Law (R.A. 10963), estate tax in the Philippines is now a flat rate of 6% of the net estate — a significant simplification from the previous multi-tier rate structure.

How Estate Tax is Computed
Gross Estate — total fair market value of all assets
minus: Allowable Deductions
minus: Standard Deduction of ₱5,000,000
minus: Family Home Deduction (up to ₱10,000,000)
Net Estate × 6% = Estate Tax Due

Note: Property valuation for estate tax purposes uses the higher of: the BIR Zonal Value, the Provincial/City Assessor's assessed value, or the Fair Market Value — whichever is highest at time of death.

Key Deadlines
  • Estate Tax Return must be filed within 1 year from the date of death
  • An extension of up to 30 days may be granted by the BIR Commissioner for meritorious reasons
  • Late filing incurs a 25% surcharge, plus 12% annual interest on unpaid tax
  • Estate Tax Amnesty (R.A. 11213 as extended) previously offered relief for estates of decedents who died on or before December 31, 2017 — consult a tax professional regarding current amnesty availability
Important Reminder

This is a general explainer only. Estate tax computation depends on the specific assets, deductions, and valuation applicable to your family's situation. Always consult a licensed accountant or tax professional for your specific computation. ALAS Property Solutions does not provide tax advice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions Heirs Ask Most

An Extra-Judicial Settlement (EJS) is a legal document signed by all heirs that distributes the estate of a deceased person without going through the courts. It is available when the deceased left no will, there are no debts, and all heirs are of legal age and in agreement. It is the fastest and most cost-effective path for most Filipino families — but all heirs must sign, and those abroad must provide an apostilled Special Power of Attorney.
Yes. An heir based abroad must execute a Special Power of Attorney (SPA) authorising a representative in the Philippines to sign on their behalf. The SPA must be notarised abroad and apostilled (or authenticated by the Philippine consulate in the country where it is executed). We can guide your family through this process — it is routine but must be done correctly.
Not in the traditional sense. The title must be transferred to the heirs' names before a property can be sold with clean title. Some arrangements allow signing a Deed of Sale simultaneously with the EJS — but this requires careful legal coordination and is generally not advisable without professional guidance. Attempting to sell without resolving the title is one of the most common and costly mistakes in inherited property transactions.
Estate tax must be settled before the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) will issue the Certificate Authorizing Registration (CAR) — which is required for title transfer. If the estate tax was not filed within the 1-year deadline, penalties and interest accrue. The BIR has periodically offered estate tax amnesty programs. We strongly recommend consulting a tax professional to assess your current liability and options before proceeding.
An uncooperative heir cannot be forced to sign an Extra-Judicial Settlement. If one heir refuses, the cooperating heirs must either reach an agreement through negotiation (often facilitated by a neutral third party) or resort to judicial partition through the courts — which is significantly slower and more costly. Early and transparent communication among heirs is the best preventive measure. We can advise on practical approaches to facilitate cooperation before escalating to legal action.
For a straightforward estate with complete documents, cooperative heirs, and no pending estate tax issues — the Extra-Judicial Settlement route typically takes 3 to 6 months from start to title transfer. The most common causes of delay are: missing documents, heirs abroad without an SPA, unpaid real property taxes, and BIR processing backlogs. Estates with complications can take significantly longer. Our Free Assessment will give you a realistic timeline based on your specific situation.
For the Extra-Judicial Settlement document itself, yes — it must be drafted by a lawyer and notarised. For the overall coordination process — gathering documents, computing estate tax, liaising with the BIR and Registry of Deeds, and managing the process timeline — a licensed real estate broker with experience in estate coordination can provide significant practical guidance and save your family considerable time and confusion.
